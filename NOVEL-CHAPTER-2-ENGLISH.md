# Chapter 2: The First Customer

The door creaked open, and a woman stepped inside, her eyes scanning the empty restaurant with obvious skepticism.

She was in her fifties, elegant in that way only Milanese women can be—impeccable coat, designer bag, shoes that probably cost more than my monthly rent. Her silver hair was styled perfectly, and she wore the expression of someone who had eaten at the best restaurants in the world and found most of them wanting.

I suddenly felt very aware of my flour-stained apron and sleepless eyes.

"Buongiorno," I managed, my Italian failing me in my nervousness. "Welcome to East & West."

She didn't smile. "You're open?"

"Yes, signora. Just opened today."

She looked around again, taking in the simple decor, the clean lines, the East Asian touches I'd added to blend with the modern Italian aesthetic. Her gaze lingered on the kitchen, visible through the open pass, where my carbonara sat steaming and perfect.

"Chinese?" she asked, her tone making it clear she wasn't sure if this was a good thing.

"Chinese-Italian fusion," I said, finding my confidence. "But today, I'm serving something traditional. Roman carbonara, made with techniques from both cultures."

Her eyebrow lifted. "Carbonara? In a Chinese restaurant?"

"Not Chinese," I corrected gently. "East & West. Two traditions, one plate."

She studied me for a long moment, and I could almost see her deciding whether to walk out. Then, to my surprise, she walked to the counter.

"Alright," she said. "Show me this carbonara. But I warn you—I grew up in Rome. I know what real carbonara tastes like."

My heart hammered against my ribs. First customer. Roman heritage. Impossibly high standards.

And the System's voice in my ear: **[This is your moment, Host! Impress her!]**

"One carbonara," I said, trying to keep my voice steady. "Would you like to see it prepared?"

She blinked, surprised. "You cook in front of customers?"

"For my first customer? Absolutely."

She actually smiled—a small, reluctant thing, but genuine. "Very well. Show me what you have."

I came around the counter and led her to the kitchen, suddenly grateful I'd spent so much time making it pristine. She watched as I moved through the steps, the System's guidance flowing through me like water.

The guanciale sizzled. The pasta boiled perfectly al dente. I cracked the eggs, mixed in the pecorino, the pepper, the timing precise to the second.

She watched, silent, her critical eyes taking in every movement. When I tossed the hot pasta into the egg mixture, creating that silky sauce without scrambling a single bit, I saw her eyebrows rise.

"Where did you learn that technique?" she asked. "That folding method—it's not traditional."

"From my grandmother," I said honestly. "She made noodles in Shanghai. The technique for mixing hot noodles with cold egg without cooking it—it's universal."

"Universal," she repeated, tasting the word. "Interesting."

I plated the carbonara simply, garnished only with a little extra pepper, and handed it to her with a fork.

"€25," I said. "First customer discount. Normally €30."

She didn't even flinch at the price, which gave me hope. Instead, she took the plate to the counter, sat on one of my stools, and took her first bite.

The restaurant fell silent. I stood in the kitchen, watching, my entire future hanging on this woman's reaction.

She chewed slowly. Methodically. Her expression gave away nothing.

Then she took another bite. And another.

"Well?" I finally asked, unable to stand the suspense.

She set down her fork and looked at me with something that might have been respect.

"It's good," she said. "Not traditional—don't call it that. But good. Better than good. The texture is... I don't know how you achieved that silkiness."

The System chimed: **[Customer satisfaction: 85%! Keep going!]**

"The technique," I said. "Hot pasta, cold egg, quick mixing. My grandmother's method."

"Your grandmother was a wise woman." She took another bite, clearly enjoying it despite herself. "I run a food blog. 'Milan Eats.' Perhaps you've heard of it?"

I had. Everyone in Milan's food scene had. Elena Rossi—the woman who could make or break a restaurant with a single review.

My mouth went dry.

"I know it," I managed. "Signora Rossi."

"Elena, please." She finished the carbonara, every last bite, and wiped her mouth delicately. "I'm going to write about this place. About this dish. But I want to try more—your fusion creations, not just Italian classics with Chinese techniques."

"I... I'm still developing the menu," I admitted. "Today was supposed to be soft opening. Just Italian to start."

"Soft opening." She laughed, a warm sound that transformed her stern face. "You served me €25 carbonara on your soft opening. Bold."

"I need bold," I said, surprising myself with my honesty. "I have one day to make €1,000, or my savings disappear."

She stared at me. "One day? €1,000? That's..."

"Impossible?" I finished for her. "Probably. But I'm going to try."

Elena Rossi stood up, reached into her designer bag, and pulled out a leather wallet. She withdrew €100 and placed it on the counter.

"For the carbonara," she said. "And for the next table's meal. Whatever you cook for them—I want to see what you do when you're not trying to impress a Roman with carbonara."

"Signora, I can't—"

"You can." She pressed the money into my hand. "And call me Elena. I'll be back tomorrow for lunch. Have something interesting ready."

She walked to the door, then paused and looked back.

"By the way—your restaurant's name. East & West. It suits you." A small smile. "I think I'm going to enjoy watching you succeed. Or fail spectacularly. Either way, it'll be interesting."

The door closed behind her, and I stood there, €100 in my hand, my heart racing.

**[DING!]**

**[First customer satisfied!]**

**[Revenue: €100 / €1,000]**

**[Progress: 10%]**

**[New ability unlocked: Recipe Synthesis]**

**[You can now combine Chinese and Italian techniques in new dishes!]**

I stared at the System panel, then at the €100 bill, then at the empty restaurant.

€100. One customer. One glowing review coming.

And I still needed €900 by midnight.

"Alright, System," I said, rolling up my sleeves tighter. "Let's get to work."

The door opened again.
